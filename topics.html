Privacy-Preserving

  • Privacy-preserving Publication of Geo-social Network Data
    TELECHARGER LE FICHIER

    Nowadays, various types of online social networks (OSN) have attracted billions of users by providing them a platform to present themselves, articulate their social circles and interact with each other, etc. This unique service makes OSNs stand out among other online services and as a consequence provides OSN owners with an unprecedented collection of diverse data. Especially, with the pervasive use of smart phones users can post new types of information, e.g., photos and videos. Thanks to GPS chips equipped, these posts can be labelled with locations and time indicating where and when they are taken. In addition, people can also share locations by publishing running tracks, posting check-ins or querying information customised to where they are (e.g., nearby friends). These location sharing social networks are usually called geo-social networks.

    Geo-social networks attract special interest from researchers as they collect both users' connections and their locations. More importantly, the spatio-temporal points, composed of locations and the sharing time, encode users' activities. A location posted on Sunday morning lying in a catholic church indicates the owner is at a religious service and is likely to be catholic. Analysis on users' social connections and locations on geo-social networks has produced abundant useful information. User connections are exploited to extract global statistics, (e.g., degree distribution, subgraph counts), and study human behaviours (e.g., communities and information diffusion patterns). Existing works have successfully used locations to infer users' regular daily activities, recommend friends and suggest trips for tourists.

    In this project, we focus on these two types of data. One is social graph, with nodes representing users and edges representing their relations. The other one consists of all posted locations by users. When a user posts a location, an edge will be created and labeled with the posting time. As users keep posting locations, we have a set of spatio-temporal points for every user, which records a trace of his chronologically ordered activities. Therefore, we model a geo-social network as a social graph with a sequence of spatio-temporal points associated with every node and call it a geo-social graph.

    The great benefits of analysis on geo-social networks make it necessary for the owners to publish more data. However, the access is still restricted for research due to the sensitivity nature of locations and social connections. Publishing them without proper protection will raise privacy concerns. Many privacy-preserving methods have thus been proposed to publish social graphs (e.g., by editing edges or synthesising graphs) and locations (e.g., obfuscation, perturbation). Recent studies illustrate that locations of two users are correlated with their friendship. Locations can be used to predict friends based on the intuition that people visiting places with similar semantics (e.g., sports centre, concerts), are more likely to be friends. Meanwhile, we can also infer a user's visited places given his friends' locations. For simplicity, we call the correlation. We notice that the correlation can also be exploited by attackers to refine their inference of sensitive information even on geo-social networks anonymised by existing privacy preserving methods. We exemplify the risk in the following. Given a social network, we anonymise the social graph by adding/removing edges to ensure that for each node, there exist at least another k-1 nodes with the same degree (i.e., k-degree anonymity) and obfuscate locations of each node into reasonably large areas. By observing the obfuscated areas of two connected nodes are overlapped neither geographically nor semantically, the attacker can decide this edge is likely to be a fake one. Similarly, the edges removed can also be recovered when two users visited places with similar semantics. The original social graph can thus be reconstructed.

    We can see that existing methods fail to protect privacy because they treat locations and social graphs independently and ignore their correlation. Meanwhile, the correlation should be preserved as it is useful for many other applications. To the best of our knowledge, no work exists to anonymise a geo-social network as a geo-social graph. In this thesis project, we aim to develop privacy preserving methods and tools for geo-social network owners to publish anonymised geo-social graphs. More specifically, the anonymised graphs should protect user privacy, e.g., no users can be re-identified. Moreover, they should remain useful for future analysis by preserving three types of features of the original graph: i) social graph's structure features, e.g., degree distributions; ii) posted locations' global features (e.g., popularity of locations) and consistency of an individual user's locations with real persons; iii) the geo-social correlation.
